Our wonderful ship is called the «SS Île de France» and was launched in 1926. The French shipping company Compagnie Générale Transatlantique signed a contract with the French government in 1916 to build four passenger ships. The first of these ships was the «SS Paris», which was put into service in 1921. The «SS Île de France» was launched on March 14, 1926, and began its first sea trials on May 29, 1926. At that time, the large ocean liners were competing for the Blue Riband, the award for the ship that could cross the Atlantic the fastest. The «SS Île de France», with a length of 214.35 meters and a width of 28 meters, was never the fastest ship with a top speed of 23.5 knots per hour (that's about 44 kilometers per hour)...but it was one of the ships with a charming interior. Everything was in the Art Deco style.

The «SS Île de France» was the world's largest new passenger ship built after the First World War.
The salon furnishings from 1926 show the lavishly gold-decorated first class salon. But the other rooms also stood out from previous ocean liners. All cabins now had beds instead of bunks. A magnificent staircase led to the exquisite dining room. There was even a carousel for children on board.
The «SS Île de France» made its maiden voyage on June 22, 1927. It was received with great applause in New York. Due to its splendor, wealthy Americans and Europeans preferred this ship for their transatlantic crossings, bringing in a lot of money for the French shipping company. The luxury was increased even further when a seaplane catapult was installed on the ship in 1928, from which flying boats could launch that flew ahead of the ship with the mail when the «SS Île de France» was about 200 nautical miles from land. This meant that the mail could be distributed on the mainland one day before the ship arrived. This also meant that at least its postal service was the fastest between Europe and the USA (until 1930, when this achievement was discontinued for cost reasons).

On September 1, 1939, the Second World War began, as a result of which civil transatlantic traffic was suspended by France.

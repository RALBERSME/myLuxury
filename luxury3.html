By the outbreak of World War II, the «SS Île de France» had completed more than 346 transatlantic crossings and transported more than 245,000 people. This made it one of the most successful ships of the 1930s.

On September 3, 1939, it set sail for New York for the last time, this time fully loaded with 1,777 passengers, far more than the ship's regular passenger capacity of 1,644. The passengers were mostly Americans fleeing war. Only two days earlier, Germany had illegally immigrated to Poland. Three hours after the Ile de France left the French port, France, like England, declared war on the Germans.

The nerves of the passengers and the crew were on edge. They could now be attacked by German submarines at any time.

On September 9, 1939, the ship finally arrived in New York Harbor. The shipping company stopped the ship's service. But the ship could not remain in the harbor permanently. So after five months it was towed to Staten Island. The crew was reduced from 800 men to just 100 men in order to reduce costs and ensure the ship's maintenance.
In March 1940, the ship was placed under the command of the British Admiralty. It was given a black-gray military paint job and loaded with 12,000 tons of war materials, tanks, oil for submarines and dismantled bombers. The ship set sail for Europe in May 1940.
In early 1941, the ship was hastily converted into a troop transport. Together with the ocean liners Queen Mary, Queen Elizabeth, Mauritania and New Amsterdam, as well as other ships, a huge convoy was formed in March 1941, which transported Australian troops to North Africa with more than 4,000 soldiers. Due to the rush, ventilation, food and sanitary facilities were not taken into account and the crossings became torture for the Australian soldiers.

In 1944, the «SS Île de France» was handed over to the Cunard White Star Line shipping company, which was preparing to transport American and Canadian troops to Europe.
The ship was thus rebuilt once again, but even though the conditions for the soldiers improved, it was still very cramped and uncomfortable. However, it played an important role in helping to prepare for the so-called D-Day. It had transported an estimated 485,000 soldiers and the once luxury ship now looked battered and worn.
